In my work with not-for-profit organisations of all shapes and sizes, I'm often reminded of the story of a young woman who, early in her relationship, decided to cook her new beau a roast chicken. They chatted away while she got everything together, but the boyfriend suddenly looked puzzled. The young woman nervously asked him if he was OK, to which he replied "yes, I'm fine, but what are you doing to that chicken?!". She looked down at the bird, including the wings and legs that she had pulled off the body and placed underneath it.
 
"What's wrong with that?" she asked, a little taken aback, "That's how I've always done it. That's how my mum does it."
 
"Weird," replies the boyfriend, "I wonder why your mum does it that way?"
 
Not wanting to lose face this early in the relationship, she suggests that they call her mum and ask.
 
"Oh, that's just the way my mum did it too – your dad always said it was a bit strange too!" was the mum's explanation, "We can ask her if you want." So they call the grandmother.
 
"Oh, I suppose I do cook it that way," says grandmother, "We only had a small roasting tin when we got married and that was the only way to get it to fit!"
 
It's a daft story, but it illustrates how so many processes become unnecessarily cumbersome. While implementing new CRMs for a wide range of charities, I've often had people look a bit put out that I've suggested revisiting processes "outside" the main CRM. But every system has its constraints, and the new CRM's constraints are almost always different to the old one's. Differences in how donations are entered into the CRM, for example, lead to upstream decisions in finance, supporter care and income processing – all before the data ever gets to the CRM. So often, in the interests of saving money during the implementation, the pragmatic decision is taken to keep the upstream processes the same.
 
This is a false economy. The amount of human-hours spent bending the results of an old upstream process into a new CRM can quickly dwarf the extra cost of revisiting the process. Easy to say that it's from a different budget line, but ultimately that's always going to mean less money going to your cause.
 
This applies particularly to your CRM, because it usually sits at the heart of your operation, but it could be equally valuable when changing any key system – marketing automation, accounts, even HR.
 
The takeaway here is that processes don't usually age well – reviewing them on a rolling schedule might not sound like the most exciting thing you've ever done, but _not_ doing it is going to cost you in the long run.
